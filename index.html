A Short Introduction to Reactive Power

Reactive power has a reputation as some sort of secret sauce for electricity, making all things possible, replete with complex numbers, admittances, reactances and whatnot, and can only be explained by analogies ranging from the useless (it’s the power moving back and forth) to the ridiculous (it’s like the foam on top of beer).

I’ve always been embarrassed that I never worked through what was really going on with reactive power. Recently, I’ve had some time to remedy that, and I’m hoping to share what I learned. Reactive power is consequence of the fact that the power grid uses alternating current. Even as the voltage and current alternate, however, they can peak at different times. This timing difference is the essence of reactive power. Anytime someone says reactive power, it’s safe to say that you can be thinking of this timing difference.

This timing difference affects the power available to consumers as well as the voltage across the grid and the stable operation of generators. It is challenging to try to describe how all this works, but rather than add to the menagerie of bad analogies – and my prior frustration with the same – I want to give it a shot. Hope it makes sense.

Voltage and Current

The two most fundamental quantities at any point in the power system are the voltage and the current. The voltage is the energy (per unit charge and measured with respect to the ground), and the current is the speed and direction at which the charge is flowing. As a result of Tesla winning his famous dispute with Edison, almost all the power system uses alternating current. This means that the current changes direction 120 times per second (60 Hertz) forming a sine wave, shown as a red line in figure 1. At the same time, the voltage also moves up and down in a sine wave, alternating between positive and negative, shown as a blue line in figure 1. As you can see from the figure, the voltage oscillations and the current oscillations do not have to be aligned. One can measure the difference in the peaks as a length of time, but generally people describe it as an angle – because, well, trigonometry turns out to be really useful. Curves that are completely aligned differ by 0 degrees, while curves that are completely out of alignment, where the maximum of one is at the same time as the minimum of the other, are said to be 180 degrees out of phase.

This angle between the voltage and the current, called the phase angle by the electrical engineers, is the underlying reality of reactive power. Put another way, reactive power is a bookkeeping device to keep track of this angle. A high level of reactive power means that the angle is closer to 90 degrees, while a negative level of reactive power means that the angle is closer to -90 degrees. (This direction is really a matter of convention, and one could also define things in the opposite way.) The amount of power available at a point on a circuit is the product of the voltage and the current. The product of these two sine waves is the [???] line in figure 1. Like the voltage and current, the power oscillates, but unlike those, it has a positive average value. This average is the power available to do work, what the engineers call real power (as opposed to reactive power). If the maximum voltage multiplied by the maximum current gives you the “apparent power”, the power that would be available if everything were in phase, the phase angle determines how much of that apparent power is actually available to do work, with less and less real power as the phase angle gets closer to plus or minus 90 degrees. The more out of phase voltage and current are, the less real power is available.
