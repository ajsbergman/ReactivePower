A Short Introduction to Reactive Power

Reactive power has a reputation as some sort of secret sauce for electricity, making all things possible, replete with complex numbers, admittances, reactances and whatnot, and can only be explained by analogies ranging from the useless (it’s the power moving back and forth) to the ridiculous (it’s like the foam on top of beer). I’ve always been embarrassed that I never worked through what was really going on with reactive power. Recently, I’ve had some time to remedy that, and I’m hoping to share what I learned. Reactive power is consequence of the fact that the power grid uses alternating current. Even as the voltage and current alternate, however, they can peak at different times. This timing difference is the essence of reactive power. Anytime someone says reactive power, it’s safe to say that you can be thinking of this timing difference. This timing difference affects the power available to consumers as well as the voltage across the grid and the stable operation of generators. It is challenging to try to describe how all this works, but rather than add to the menagerie of bad analogies – and my prior frustration with the same – I want to give it a shot. Hope it makes sense.
